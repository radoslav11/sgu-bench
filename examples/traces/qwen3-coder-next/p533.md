To solve this problem, we need to determine the minimum number of dice (height of the tower) such that the sum of the dots on all outer surfaces of the tower equals a given number \( n \). The key insight is recognizing the mathematical relationship between the number of dice and the sum of the exposed faces.

### Approach
1. **Problem Analysis**: 
   - Each die in the tower contributes 14 to the total sum of the side faces (since the four side faces of a die always sum to 14, regardless of rotation).
   - The top face of the top die and the bottom face of the bottom die can each be any value from 1 to 6, contributing between 2 and 12 to the total sum.
   - Therefore, the total sum \( n \) for a tower of height \( h \) must satisfy \( 14h + 2 \leq n \leq 14h + 12 \).

2. **Key Insight**:
   - For a given \( n \), we need to find the smallest \( h \) such that \( n \) lies in the interval \([14h + 2, 14h + 12]\).
   - Rearranging the inequality, we get \( h \geq \lceil (n - 12)/14 \rceil \) and \( h \leq \lfloor (n - 2)/14 \rfloor \).
   - The minimal valid \( h \) is \( \max(1, \lceil (n - 12)/14 \rceil) \), provided this \( h \) is within the valid range \( [1, \lfloor (n - 2)/14 \rfloor] \).

3. **Algorithm Selection**:
   - Compute \( h_{\text{min}} = \lceil (n - 12)/14 \rceil \). If this value is zero or negative, set \( h_{\text{min}} \) to 1 (since the tower must have at least one die).
   - Compute \( h_{\text{max}} = \lfloor (n - 2)/14 \rfloor \).
   - If \( h_{\text{min}} \leq h_{\text{max}} \), then \( h_{\text{min}} \) is the answer; otherwise, no solution exists, and we return -1.
