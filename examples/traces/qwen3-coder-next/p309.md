To solve this problem, we need to find the minimal side length \( d \) such that three axis-aligned squares of side \( d \) can cover all given points on a Cartesian plane. The solution involves efficiently determining the smallest \( d \) by considering different ways to partition the points into up to three segments, each covered by a single square.

### Approach
1. **Problem Analysis**: The problem requires covering all points with three squares of minimal side length. The squares must be axis-aligned, meaning their sides are parallel to the coordinate axes. The key insight is that the optimal solution can be derived by partitioning the points into contiguous segments when sorted by either the x or y coordinate. Each segment is then covered by one square, and the side length \( d \) for each segment is the maximum of the width (difference in x-coordinates) and height (difference in y-coordinates) of the points in the segment.

2. **Algorithm Selection**:
   - **Sorting and Preprocessing**: For both x and y coordinates, sort the points and precompute arrays `left` and `right`:
     - `left[i]` stores the minimal \( d \) required to cover points from index 0 to \( i \) (inclusive) when sorted by the current coordinate.
     - `right[i]` stores the minimal \( d \) required to cover points from index \( i \) to \( n-1 \) (inclusive).
   - **Sparse Table for Range Queries**: To efficiently compute the minimal \( d \) for any segment during ternary search, we use a sparse table for range minimum and maximum queries on the other coordinate (y when sorted by x, and x when sorted by y).
   - **Ternary Search for Optimal Split**: For each possible split into three segments, we use ternary search to find the optimal split points that minimize the maximum \( d \) among the three segments.

3. **Complexity Analysis**:
   - **Sorting**: Sorting the points takes \( O(n \log n) \).
   - **Preprocessing Arrays**: Computing `left` and `right` arrays takes \( O(n) \).
   - **Sparse Table Construction**: Building the sparse table takes \( O(n \log n) \).
   - **Ternary Search**: For each of the \( O(n) \) splits, ternary search runs in \( O(\log n) \) per split, leading to \( O(n \log n) \) total time.
   - **Overall Complexity**: The total time complexity is \( O(n \log n) \), which is efficient for \( n \leq 20,000 \).
